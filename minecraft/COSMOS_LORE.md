# COSMOS LORE
### Narrative Archive — Cosmos Enterprises

> This document preserves the narrative vision of Cosmos as it was conceived.
> It is a reference, not a blueprint. These ideas belong to Cosmos Enterprises.

## This Game's Relationship to Cosmos

**Antithesis Conquering Simulator is not a retelling of Cosmos.** It is a character study — an expansion of aspects of the Cosmos universe that never got fully realized. It does not require Cosmos to work. It does not explain its own lineage. It simply lets these characters exist fully for the first time.

The Cosmos connection is felt, not stated. Players who never knew Cosmos will find a tight, intense roguelite with a mysterious and oppressive final boss. Players who did know Cosmos will recognize something — and that recognition is its own reward, unremarked upon by the game itself.

**THE PLANT appears in this game as the final boss of every run.** Its role here is not narrative. It is atmospheric and mechanical — the unavoidable ceiling of every run, a presence that confirms the world is not random but authored. It does not explain itself. It does not need to.

---

## Overview

Cosmos was designed with a narrative that revealed itself in layers — a first run that felt complete, and a second run that recontextualized everything that came before it. The story was never delivered through exposition. It lived in the world, the bosses, and the moment the player realized they had been playing a different game than they thought.

---

## The First Run

Cosmos crash-lands on a planet. The goal is simple: **ascend Cosmos Mountain, repair the ship, escape.**

Seven areas stand between Cosmos and the summit. Each one is a territory — not a level, but a *place* that grew from the personality of the boss who claims it. The world is built from the inside out.

At the top of the mountain: **THE PLANT.**

### The Ending — First Run

THE PLANT's area crowns the mountain. When Cosmos arrives, THE PLANT does not attack.

It presents itself as **a plant man in a suit.** Composed. Civil. It talks.

Then the battle begins. THE PLANT transforms — sheds the suit, sheds the form — and becomes something enormous and biological. **A massive plant tentacle monster.** The final boss in the truest sense.

Cosmos kills it.

**Then Cosmos wakes up.**

Right next to the broken ship. New save file. The game has reset.

## The Second Run

Everything is the same. The mountain is the same. The areas are the same.

But something is **wrong.** The world is more corrupted. More hostile. Darker in ways that are hard to name at first and impossible to ignore by the end.

### The Truth

The world — the planet, the areas, the bosses, the atmosphere — is in some way **an extension of THE PLANT.** It did not just inhabit the mountain. It *was* the world, or had grown through it so completely that the distinction had ceased to matter.

And Cosmos — *(WIP. The full scope of this thread was never resolved, but the implication is that Cosmos's relationship to the Plant and the world is deeper than "crash-landed outsider." What Cosmos is, and whether they were ever truly separate from the world they were trying to escape, was the central unresolved question of the narrative.)*

### Malice

Malice was the game's **passive antagonist** in the first run — present, opposed to Cosmos in some way, but never the primary threat. A figure whose role in the first run was defined by what they *didn't* do as much as what they did.

In the second run, Malice becomes an **ally.** The shared enemy — THE PLANT revealed and unkilled — reframes everything. Malice and Cosmos ascend the mountain together.

THE PLANT is killed. For good, this time.

## THE PLANT — Character Notes

- Presents as civilized, contained, almost bureaucratic — a plant man in a suit
- The suit is a performance; what's underneath is ancient and vast
- Its territory at the top of Cosmos Mountain is the culmination of everything the world has been building toward
- It does not fight until it has spoken — and when it speaks, it is not threatening. That is what makes it threatening.
- Its true form is biological horror: tentacles, scale, something that was never meant to be seen
- **It is the author of worlds** — not a ruler, not a conqueror, but the thing that writes the conditions everyone else lives inside. Always one step above. Always already ahead. A being whose ways are beyond comprehension.

The contrast between the suited figure and the monster underneath is the point. THE PLANT is the most dangerous thing in the world precisely because it knows how to look like it isn't.

### THE PLANT in Antithesis Conquering Simulator

THE PLANT is the final boss of every run. This is what that means in practice:

- It does not arrive. You reach it — or rather, you reach the layer of the world that is entirely, purely *its*.
- It never fully appears. What you fight is its influence, its architecture, its extensions. The thing orchestrating it is somewhere beyond the frame of the encounter, watching, unbothered.
- It is the only enemy in any run that actually *sees* Antithesis. Every other threat is a native mechanical force doing what it does. THE PLANT is aware. That distinction is the horror.
- The businessman presentation carries. There is a moment of stillness before everything goes wrong — composed, almost polite. The transition is where the fear lives.
- Killing it ends the run. But the next run's planets may be slightly different. The ship on the crashlanding may have shifted. Nothing is confirmed. Nothing is explained. It is simply there for the player to notice — or not.

THE PLANT does not lose because it is defeated. It loses, if it loses at all, for reasons it has already accounted for.

## Antithesis — Role in the Narrative

Antithesis was **Area 6** — the penultimate boss, the last major obstacle before THE PLANT.

Its territory: shattered glass, muted damp greens, the moon, metal, toxicity. The most hostile environment in the game, by design. An unfun place to be — and deliberately so.

Antithesis is a **shapeshifter** with no fixed form. For the fight against Cosmos, it mirrored them — same silhouette, wrong in every detail. It carried a **robot arm** and **mechanical wings**, which Cosmos claimed upon killing it. These became part of Cosmos's kit for the final ascent.

Antithesis's story ends at its defeat. It was never more than what it was: **the most powerful, most menacing, most terrifying thing Cosmos had encountered.** A force of nature. That was always enough.

## What Was Never Finished

Cosmos was a large vision that outgrew the moment it was conceived in. The following threads were alive but unresolved at the time development stalled:

- The full nature of Cosmos's connection to THE PLANT and the world
- Malice's complete arc across both runs
- The precise mechanics of the world's corruption in the second run
- Whether the second run's ending offered resolution or a third layer

These are preserved here as open questions, not failures. They are the shape of what Cosmos was reaching toward.
